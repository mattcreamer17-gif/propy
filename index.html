Hypothesis

Three things we think are true right now.

  1. 01

    Acquired-company integration is eating calendar weeks.

    Each shop ships with Qualia, SoftPro, RamQuest, or ResWare, different state formats, and staff trained on local process. Getting Avery to operate on top of all of them without breaking per-state compliance is where the roll-up thesis decides itself.

  2. 02

    Edge-case handling is where the liability lives.

    The 40% reduction means 60% still needs a human, and that 60% is the gnarly work: liens, estate issues, HOA payoff disputes, odd title defects. Avery needs a clean escalation layer with audit trails.

  3. 03

    Regulatory surface is expanding faster than internal process.

    MiCA in the EU. State-by-state US licensing (FL, AZ, CO, now CA). Each jurisdiction adds rules to encode. A formal context layer lets one agent operate across jurisdictions without bespoke retraining.

The frame

Treat agent context as an architecture, not a prompt.

Most teams building agentic systems pick a model, wrap it in a prompt, and call it a product. That works until a second acquired title company arrives and the prompt has to account for state-specific recording rules, different HOA communication norms, and a different legacy platform. At that point the prompt turns into a 4,000-word document no one wants to touch, and the last engineer who understood it leaves for another startup.

Our take: agent context should be layered, versioned, and inspectable. Each layer has its own tools, its own audit trail, and its own escalation path. The orchestration layer is what decides which problem goes where.
